MILKY WAY GALAXY

MOON

The Moon formed around 4.5 billion years ago, likely as a result of a massive collision between Earth and a Mars-sized body called Theia. The debris from this impact eventually came together to form the Moon. In its early history,The Moon was volcanically active, and its surface was heavily bombarded by asteroids,creating the many craters we see today. Over time, the Moon’s gravitational pull has played a keyrole in stabilizing Earth’s tilt and creating ocean tides.It has remained a constant presence in our sky.first visited by humans during the Apollo 11 mission in 1969.

STARS

Stars began forming over 13 billion years ago, shortly after the Big Bang, from vast clouds of gas and dust in space. Gravity caused these clouds to collapse, increasing pressure and temperature until nuclear fusion ignited in their cores. This fusion process, where hydrogen turns into helium, powers stars and produces the light and heat they emit. Over their lifetimes,stars create heavier elements and influence the formation of planets and galaxies.They die,depending on their size,they may become white dwarfs,neutron stars, or black holes,continuing to shape the universe long after their light fades.

SUN

The Sun formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a giant cloud of gas and dust in space, known as a solar nebula. Gravity pulled most of this material to the center, where intense pressure and heat triggered nuclear fusion, turning hydrogen into helium and releasing massive energy. This process continues today, making the Sun the main source of light and heat for our solar system. Over billions of years, the Sun has remained stable, supporting life on Earth. In the distant future, it will expand into a red giant and eventually shrink a white dwarf, marking the end of its life cycle.

EARTH

Earth's history spans approximately 4.5 billion years, beginning with its formation from a massive cloud of gas and dust left over from the birth of the Sun. Through a process called accretion, particles collided and merged, gradually forming a planet. In its early stages, Earth was a molten sphere, but as it cooled, a solid crust formed on the surface. Intense volcanic activity released gases, which helped create the early atmosphere. Water vapor condensed to form oceans, making Earth one of the only known planets capable of supporting life. Life is thought to have first appeared in the oceans around 3.8 billion years ago as microscopic, single-celled organisms. Over the next few billion years, these simple life forms evolved into more complex organisms through the process of natural selection. Major evolutionary milestones include the development of photosynthesis, which added oxygen to the atmosphere, and the emergence of multicellular organisms. Around 541 million years ago, during the Cambrian Explosion, a remarkable diversification of life occurred, giving rise to many of the major animal groups we know today. Earth has also experienced several mass extinctions, such as the one that wiped out the dinosaurs about 66 million years ago, likely due to an asteroid impact. Each extinction event reshaped life on the planet and allowed new species to evolve. Geological processes like plate tectonics have continuously altered the continents and ocean basins, shaping Earth’s surface over time. Human beings appeared relatively recently, about 300,000 years ago, and have since significantly influenced the planet's ecosystems and climate. Earth's history is a story of constant change, driven by both natural forces and the life that has evolved upon it.

PLANET

The history of the planets in our solar system is a vast and complex story that began over 4.6 billion years ago, when a massive cloud of gas and dust—known as the solar nebula—collapsed under its own gravity, possibly triggered by a nearby supernova. At the center of this collapsing cloud, the Sun was born, and around it, the remaining material flattened into a spinning protoplanetary disk. From this disk, the planets gradually formed through a process called accretion, where dust particles stuck together to form planetesimals, which collided and merged to become protoplanets. The four inner planets—Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars—formed from heavier elements and became rocky due to the intense heat near the Sun. Farther out, the gas giants—Jupiter and Saturn—accumulated massive envelopes of hydrogen and helium, while the ice giants—Uranus and Neptune—formed in colder regions and incorporated more ices like water, ammonia, and methane. Each planet developed its own unique characteristics through interactions with the Sun, collisions with other bodies, and dynamic internal processes. For example, Earth's geological activity and water enabled the emergence of life, while Mars experienced massive climate changes that may have once supported water on its surface. Jupiter’s immense gravity influenced the orbits of many bodies, even shaping the asteroid belt, and may have shielded Earth from excessive asteroid impacts. Over billions of years, planetary migration, bombardments (such as the Late Heavy Bombardment), and internal evolution (like volcanism, tectonics, and magnetic fields) have all shaped the solar system into what we observe today. Despite our advanced understanding, many mysteries remain about the planets’ early development and their long-term evolution, making planetary history an ongoing subject of exploration and discovery.
